The Origin

It started with a personal frustration.

Like most people, I wanted to track my money. I tried every popular app — Walnut, Money Manager, YNAB, even Splitwise. They all had the same pattern: open app, tap the plus button, select category, enter amount, add note, save. Six steps to log a single cup of coffee.

So I started doing something unusual: I logged my expenses by talking to ChatGPT. I'd just type "spent 240 on coffee at Starbucks" and it would understand me. No dropdowns. No categories. No friction. It felt natural — because that's how I already talk about money.

But ChatGPT has no memory. No dashboard. No insights. It was a brilliant interaction model trapped in the wrong container. That gap became the idea for Justlog.

"Everyone I talked to had the same problem: they wanted to track money, but they always quit the apps. The apps made them feel like accountants, not people."
— Pavan Jangid, Designer & Founder
Research & Discovery

What I found when I actually asked people.

I spent time talking to a range of people — students, working professionals, couples managing shared expenses, and small business owners. I wasn't running formal usability tests. I was having real conversations about how they relate to money and why every app they'd tried had failed them.

Three patterns emerged consistently across every conversation:

Finding 01

The habit breaks at the form

Almost everyone starts with good intentions, logs for a few days, then stops. The friction isn't remembering to log — it's the form itself. Dropdowns, categories, and fields feel like admin work, not a personal habit.

Finding 02

Categories are a lie we tell ourselves

People don't think in "Food & Dining" or "Transportation." They think "I grabbed lunch with Priya" or "Ola to the airport." Forced categorisation adds cognitive load and never truly fits how people actually spend.

Finding 03

Group expenses are a social nightmare

Splitting expenses is stressful. WhatsApp messages, screenshots, mental arithmetic. Existing apps like Splitwise feel clinical — they track debt, not context. People wanted to log shared expenses the way they'd tell a friend.

Design Philosophy

Three principles that governed everything.

Before touching Figma, I wrote down three principles. Every single feature decision — what to build, how to build it, what to cut — was judged against these.

Zero friction at the point of logging

The moment you have to think about how to log something, the habit is already dying. The core interaction had to be as fast as sending a WhatsApp message. Anything that slowed that down was a design failure.

The AI works for the user, invisibly

AI should feel like magic, not machinery. Users shouldn't need to learn prompt syntax or see loading spinners for 3 seconds. Justlog's AI parses intent, fills in context, and confirms — all in a single smooth moment.

Group money should feel like conversation

Shared expenses carry social weight — the awkwardness of who owes what, the context of why. The Spaces feature was designed to preserve that human context, not strip it away into a ledger of debts.

Feature Design

Every feature, deeply considered.

These aren't feature descriptions. They're design decisions — the thinking behind why each feature exists, what alternatives were rejected, and what the user experience had to feel like.

Core Interaction

Natural language logging — the heart of the product

The primary input is a text field that accepts anything. "Paid 450 for dinner at Social" — Justlog extracts the amount (₹450), the category (Food), the merchant (Social), and timestamps it. No fields. No dropdowns.

The AI uses Gemini to parse intent in real time. But the critical design decision was: always confirm before saving. A parsed preview appears — the user sees exactly what got extracted, can edit any field, then confirms. This single confirmation step solved trust. Users don't feel out of control even when the AI is doing the work.

Quick chips sit below the input for the most common transactions — "Coffee ☕", "Lunch 🍱", "Auto 🛺". One tap, pre-filled. For users who log the same things every day, it becomes muscle memory within a week.

Voice Input

Voice logging — for when your hands are full

The most natural moment to log an expense is right when it happens — stepping out of a cab, carrying groceries, at a restaurant. Those are exactly the moments when typing is awkward.

Voice input was built not as a novelty, but as the primary input for users on the move. Speak "spent three hundred on petrol," and Gemini's speech-to-text combined with the same NLP pipeline handles the rest. The same confirmation step applies — so voice doesn't feel like a "fire and forget" risk.

The key design challenge was latency. AI parsing adds time. The solution was optimistic UI — the input shows the transcribed text immediately while parsing runs in the background, so users see progress without waiting for a spinner.

Spaces

Spaces — group expenses without the awkwardness

Spaces are shared logging environments — a flat, a trip, a couple's account. Any member can log an expense. Everyone sees the full picture. No one has to chase a WhatsApp thread to remember who paid for dinner.

The fundamental design question was: should Spaces track debts (like Splitwise) or just shared context? I chose shared context. Justlog doesn't tell you who owes what — it tells you what your group spent and who logged it. Why? Because debt-tracking adds social tension. Context-sharing builds collective awareness.

When someone logs in a Space, other members see it in real time. Approvals are required for certain actions — a careful system designed to prevent accidental edits while keeping the experience fluid. Pending request cards appear at the top of the feed, dismissible immediately after action.

Stories

Stories — insights that actually make sense

Most finance apps show you charts. Pie charts of categories you already know you overspent in. Justlog's Stories feature was designed to surface insight, not just data.

Stories are AI-generated summaries of your spending behaviour — written in plain language, not finance-speak. "You spent 40% more on food this week than last. Most of it was weekday lunches." That's useful. A pie chart labelled "Food & Dining: 38%" is not.

The Stories interface borrows the familiar Instagram story format — full-screen cards you swipe through. The design decision here was intentional: familiar navigation patterns reduce cognitive load when the content itself is new.

Subscription & Paywall

The paywall — designed with intent, not desperation

For new users, Justlog shows a paywall without a close button. This was the most debated decision in the entire product. Conventional UX wisdom says always give users an exit. I disagreed — and here's why.

Justlog's free tier is fully functional. The paywall isn't a "pay to see anything" gate — it's a trial moment. New users who see the paywall haven't logged a single transaction yet. The paywall explains what they get, offers a 7-day free trial, and lets them start immediately with no credit card. The "no X button" forces a moment of decision — try it, or leave.

The data-backed reasoning: users who skip onboarding paywalls and go straight to the free tier almost never convert. Users who start a trial experience the product's value before making any decision. The paywall is designed for the user's long-term benefit, not just the business's.

The Hard Call

The decision that required conviction.

Every product has a moment where the designer has to choose between safe and right. For Justlog, it was the decision to remove all predefined categories.

Every money app uses categories.
Justlog doesn't.

The conventional approach in personal finance is to force users into a taxonomy: Food, Transport, Entertainment, Shopping. It gives apps something to chart. It gives users something to blame themselves for. But it fundamentally misrepresents how people actually think about money.

The safe decision was to add categories. Users expect them. Investors understand them. Competitors have them. Without categories, the first feedback in testing was "how do I organise my expenses?"

The answer is: you don't. The AI does. Gemini infers context from how you describe an expense. "Grabbed lunch with the team at Bombay Canteen" becomes a Food entry tagged with a merchant and a social context — without you selecting a single dropdown.

I held the line. Adding categories would have made Justlog easier to explain and harder to love. The product's entire value proposition is that it understands you. Adding a taxonomy to manage would have made users feel like they were back in every other app they'd already quit.

This decision was validated in testing — users who pushed back on "no categories" in week one were the same users who said they'd never go back in week three.

The Details

Small decisions that add up to trust.

Design leadership isn't just about the big strategic calls. It's about caring about the details so deeply that users feel safe in the product without knowing why. Here are some of the micro-decisions that shaped the final experience.

Detail 01

Pending request cards dismiss immediately

When you approve or deny a request in a Space, the card disappears instantly — no loading state, no delay. Optimistic UI keeps the experience feeling responsive and trustworthy, even though a server round-trip is happening in the background.

Detail 02

The trial banner persists across sessions

If you start a trial and close the app, the trial banner is still there when you come back. This was a deliberate retention decision — the trial's ticking clock should always be visible, creating the right amount of urgency without being aggressive.

Detail 03

Export filters at human timescales

Exporting data offers 1 Month, 3 Months, 6 Months, 1 Year, and All Time. Not "Q1 2026" or "last 30 days." People think in months and years, not fiscal quarters. The filter reflects how users actually think about their financial history.

Detail 04

Space transactions scoped correctly in PDFs

When exporting a Space's transaction history, the PDF only includes transactions that belong to that specific Space — not the user's entire transaction log. This sounds obvious but required careful DB-level scoping and a deliberate design decision about data ownership.

Detail 05

Feature card padding — optically balanced

On the landing page, feature cards have equal top and bottom padding defined in CSS — but with a tall phone screenshot inside, the text column visually floated to the centre, making the bottom look padded more. Fixed by aligning to flex-start, not centre. The final result feels equal even though the values are technically equal.

Detail 06

The hero CTA hover was broken — then fixed properly

CSS animations with animation-fill-mode: both take ownership of the transform property, breaking hover effects after the animation completes. The fix: listen for the animationend event and explicitly reset the animation, freeing transform for hover. A detail that took 20 minutes to find and 2 lines to fix — but completely changes the feel of the first interaction.

Strategic Restraint

What I chose not to build.

Product leadership is as much about what you say no to as what you ship. These are features that were considered, discussed, and deliberately cut — and the reasoning behind each decision.

✕

Bank account sync

Automatic import from bank statements sounds like a power feature. But it destroys the habit-building loop — if the app logs for you, you never build a relationship with your spending. Manual logging is intentional by design.

✕

Budget limits & alerts

Budget alerts make people feel guilty, not empowered. The goal of Justlog is awareness — seeing where your money goes — not policing behaviour. Judgement-free logging is a core brand value.

✕

Predefined expense categories

Already covered above — but worth restating. Categories are the most-requested feature and the feature most likely to undermine the product's core promise. The answer is always no.

✕

Debt tracking in Spaces

Splitwise-style "who owes who" was an early consideration for Spaces. Cut because it introduces social friction. Justlog tracks shared context, not debt. The product's role is to inform, not arbitrate.

✕

Recurring transactions

Auto-logging monthly subscriptions removes another point of awareness. Justlog wants you to notice every transaction. Automation is the enemy of financial mindfulness at this stage of the product.

✕

Social / sharing features

Finance is personal. Sharing spending habits publicly — even gamified — would change behaviour in unhealthy ways. Money is not a leaderboard. Spaces exist for genuine shared contexts, not social performance.

Beyond Design

I didn't just design it. I shipped it.

Most designers hand off to engineering. I didn't have that option — and I'm better for it. Building the product myself gave me a level of design precision that's impossible to achieve through specs and handoffs alone. When I discovered a hover state was broken in production, I fixed it. When the PDF export was pulling the wrong data, I debugged the database query.

This is what "end to end" actually means: not just designing screens, but being responsible for the product in front of a real user on a real device.

Reflection

What this project taught me.

Justlog was not a controlled exercise. It was a real product, with real decisions, real technical constraints, and a real user at the end of every flow. That changes everything about how you design.

Leadership insight

The best design decisions are the ones you can defend under pressure

Every feature in Justlog was questioned at some point — by testing, by feedback, by my own second-guessing. The ones that survived are the ones with a clear principle behind them. "No categories" held because the reasoning was solid, not because I was attached to it.

Design insight

Shipping breaks assumptions that Figma never will

The hover animation bug, the PDF scoping issue, the Lenis scroll conflict with GSAP — none of these would have appeared in a prototype. Real engineering reveals design assumptions you didn't know you were making. Building taught me to design for production, not for screens.

Product insight

What you don't build defines your product as much as what you do

Every "no" in Justlog's product decisions was a statement about what the product stands for. No categories, no debt tracking, no bank sync — each cut sharpened the product's identity. Restraint is a design skill, not a limitation.

Personal insight

The fastest way to become a better designer is to own the entire problem

When you design, build, and support a product yourself, there is no one to hand an ambiguous brief to. Every unclear spec is your fault. Every missed edge case is your responsibility. That accountability produces better design than any review process I've experienced.
